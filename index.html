Oxygenation inadequate
Increase P-high by 2 cmH₂O (max 30) or extend T-high by 0.5–1s. FiO₂ increase is a temporizing measure only.
Increase P-high or extend T-high
Ventilation inadequate (CO₂ retention)
Shorten T-low to increase release frequency or increase P-high to P-low pressure gradient. Do not extend T-low beyond 0.8s.
Shorten T-low or increase P-high gradient
Weaning from APRV
Extend T-high progressively (4→6→8→10→12s) while reducing P-high gradually. At P-high ≤16 and T-high ≥12s, consider extubation or PSV transition.
Drop-and-stretch: extend T-high, reduce P-high
This tab describes what each ventilator mode does and when it is used. For IBW-based tidal volume targets, driving pressure, and initial settings, use the Calculator tab. APRV has its own dedicated settings calculator under the APRV / bilevel tab.
AC/VC — assist control, volume controlled
Volume-targeted · flow-limited
Delivers a set tidal volume at a set rate. Patient can trigger additional breaths, each receiving the full set Vt. Most common mode for initial intubation. Guarantees minute ventilation but can generate high pressures if compliance drops.
Go-to for initial intubation and acute respiratory failure
AC/PC — assist control, pressure controlled
Pressure-targeted · volume-variable
Delivers a set inspiratory pressure at a set rate. Tidal volume varies with compliance and resistance. Limits barotrauma risk but does not guarantee Vt — monitor closely if lung mechanics change.
Preferred when pressure limitation is the priority
SIMV — synchronized intermittent mandatory ventilation
Mandatory breaths + spontaneous window
Delivers mandatory breaths synchronized to patient effort, allowing spontaneous breaths between. Largely supplanted by daily SBTs for weaning. Can increase work of breathing at low mandatory rates.
Falling out of favor — SBT + PSV more effective for weaning
PSV — pressure support ventilation
Spontaneous mode · patient-triggered only
Augments every patient-triggered breath with a set pressure boost. No mandatory rate. PS of 5–8 cmH₂O approximates ETT resistance compensation. Apnea backup ventilation must be set.
Primary weaning mode — PS 5–8 cmH₂O for SBT
PRVC — pressure-regulated volume control
Dual-control · volume target, pressure-limited
Targets a set Vt with the lowest necessary pressure. Vent adjusts pressure breath-to-breath based on measured compliance. Available as AutoFlow (Dräger) or PRVC (Maquet/Getinge).
Volume guarantee with pressure protection — best of both
APRV — airway pressure release ventilation
Time-cycled · pressure-limited · inverse ratio
Maintains lung recruitment at P-high for most of the cycle (T-high), with brief releases to P-low (T-low) for CO₂ clearance. Patient breathes spontaneously throughout. Inverse I:E ratio promotes alveolar recruitment. Requires careful T-low titration at the bedside.
ARDS with recruitable lung — spontaneous breathing preserved

△ High pressure alarm
  • Coughing / dyssynchrony — assess sedation, comfort, synchrony
  • Secretions / mucus plug — suction; consider bronchoscopy if persistent
  • Bronchospasm — auscultate for wheeze; administer bronchodilator
  • Biting the tube — bite block; sedation adjustment
  • Pneumothorax — absent unilateral breath sounds, tracheal deviation, hypotension — CXR/ultrasound urgently
  • Right mainstem intubation — absent left breath sounds; pull back ETT, verify position
  • Decreased compliance — consolidation, effusion, worsening ARDS, abdominal compartment syndrome
  • Kinked or occluded circuit — inspect tubing and water traps
△ Low pressure / low volume alarm
  • Circuit disconnect — check all connections from vent to patient
  • Cuff leak — audible leak; check cuff pressure (target 20–30 cmH₂O)
  • ETT displacement / accidental extubation — verify position; auscultate; CXR
  • Bronchopleural fistula — persistent large leak; consult surgery/pulm
  • Loose circuit connection — inspect water traps, humidifier, Y-piece
● Apnea alarm
  • Over-sedation — most common; assess RASS; consider dose reduction
  • Central apnea — neurologic event; assess neuro status; consider imaging
  • Post-hyperventilation apnea — PaCO₂ below respiratory threshold; check ABG
  • Trigger sensitivity mismatch — adjust trigger threshold
  • PSV/SIMV with inadequate backup rate — verify apnea ventilation settings
  • True respiratory arrest — assess, call for help, ensure backup ventilation active

△ If any of the following develop or persist after 1–2 hours of optimal NIV, escalation to invasive ventilation should be considered. Clinical judgment always applies.
Respiratory / gas exchange failure
  • Worsening or persistently low pH despite 1–2h (pH <7.25 or not trending toward 7.35)
  • Rising PaCO₂ on serial ABGs despite appropriate settings
  • Persistent hypoxemia — SpO₂ not meeting target on FiO₂ ≥0.6 with optimal EPAP
  • Sustained RR >35 — increasing work of breathing despite support
Hemodynamic / neurologic failure
  • Hemodynamic instability — hypotension, arrhythmia, shock
  • Deteriorating mental status — agitation, combativeness, decreasing GCS
  • Inability to protect airway — absent gag, unable to manage secretions
  • Cardiac or respiratory arrest — immediate intubation
Practical / tolerance failure
  • Excessive secretions not manageable with NIV in place
  • Intolerance / severe agitation despite optimization
  • Uncontrollable leak — facial anatomy or trauma preventing adequate seal
Early recognition matters. A patient working hard and not improving at 1 hour warrants early team discussion — not a wait-and-see approach.

Clinical uses
Inhaled epoprostenol is a potent pulmonary vasodilator used as adjunct in severe ARDS with refractory hypoxemia (improves V/Q matching), pulmonary arterial hypertension crises, right heart failure with pulmonary hypertension, and post-cardiac surgery pulmonary HTN. Inhaled delivery minimizes systemic hypotension compared to IV prostaglandins.
Adjunct for refractory hypoxemia and pulmonary HTN
Administration notes
Delivered via continuous nebulization into the inspiratory limb of the ventilator circuit. Requires a dedicated nebulizer port. Ice bath or refrigeration maintains stability — check institutional protocol. Solution should be clear to slightly yellow; discard if discolored. Half-life is 2–3 minutes — effects dissipate rapidly on discontinuation.
Continuous neb into inspiratory limb · pharmacy verifies all doses
△ ET tube drug administration is a last resort when IV access is unavailable. Absorption is unpredictable and inferior to IV. Establish IV or IO access as soon as possible.
Administration technique: Dilute dose in 5–10 mL NS or sterile water. Instill directly into ETT via suction catheter passed beyond ETT tip. Follow immediately with 5 rapid bag ventilations to aerosolize and disperse into the lungs. Resume normal ventilation.

Drugs compatible with nebulization via ETT
Bronchodilators (in-line nebulization)
Albuterol, ipratropium, and Duoneb can all be delivered via in-line nebulizer in the ventilator circuit. Place nebulizer 15–30 cm proximal to the ETT in the inspiratory limb. MDI with in-line spacer is preferred when available — delivers more consistent dose than SVN in ventilated patients. Use SVN flow of 6–8 L/min.
In-line neb or MDI spacer — both effective in ventilated patients
Racemic epinephrine via ETT
0.5 mL of 2.25% racemic epinephrine in 3 mL NS can be instilled directly into the ETT for severe bronchospasm unresponsive to standard bronchodilators, followed by 5 rapid bag ventilations. Off-label rescue technique — use only when standard routes have failed. Monitor for tachycardia and hypertension.
Rescue technique for refractory bronchospasm — monitor vitals
Epoprostenol (continuous in-line)
Delivered continuously into the inspiratory limb via dedicated nebulizer port. Not instilled as a bolus — requires continuous pharmacy-prepared infusion. See Epoprostenol tab for dosing calculator.
Continuous in-line only — never as a bolus instillation
IO access is preferred over ET tube drug administration. Intraosseous access provides reliable and predictable drug delivery comparable to IV. If IV fails during a code, establish IO before resorting to ET tube administration.
